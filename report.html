from measuring social welfare to the potential of formalizing care services

# Increasing trend of dependent regardless demographic switch
The betterment of life quality supporting system prolongs the life expectancy of the population nowadays. In parallel, the fertility rate shows a declining trend. These demographic changes have reduced the aggregate burden of child rearing, while increasing the burden of elder care.
For example, I attached trends of dependent population from Japan, US, and Sweden. Across all three countries, the under-15 share has declined steadily while the 65+ share has risen. This pattern expected to continue through 2040.

Measuring household time allocation

  • beneficial to understand how tasks and resources are allocated within a household.
  • The most common method is by utilizing time use surveys which usually collected by national bureaus or agencies
  • Miller and Sedai (2021) broaden the data source by using panel-time diaries to account the effect of time-varying variables

(Older) Household heads had to reduce time allocated for other than caregiving

  • For every additional hour of caregiving activities, study from Miller and Sedai (2021) shows that allocated time for work, leisure, home production, and personal care are reduced.
  • The panel time-use data shows that women have a greater time reduction on caring for self rather than men, thus inflicting health degradation.
  • We are also interested if the reduction of other life aspects because of non-spousal care also applies to younger household heads.

# Treating PUDO trips as unpaid caregiving activities can be a first step to understand relationship between providing care and social welfare

  • Using Becker’s (1965) time allocation framework, we can see that the increasing care needs (increasing duration of caregiving) of a household member will effectively raises providers’ marginal benefit of giving care.
  • Caregiving activity includes all unpaid physical, medical, and school related assistance to others which often is a time intensive task (Miller and Sedai, 2021)

Potential of increased total economic production by putting nonmarket care-related activities into calculation

  • Folbre and Nelson (2000) argues the economy will seem to grow if we put value on non-market family-related or care-related activities and include them in calculation
  • Robert Eisner (1989) adds that the working hours of nonmarket activities accounts a significant amount of 40% to 60% from the total US output value
  • The Europe Union made effort to develop alternative income calculation by utilizing time-use data of activities
  • Calculation can also pushed further by taking account the value of household capital and raw materials (Ironmonger, 1996)

The Idea of Gross Economic Product by Ironmonger (1996)

\[GEP = GHP + GMP\]

Where:

\[GHP = W_H + K_H = \left(\sum_{i=1}^{n} h_i \cdot w\right) + \left(K_{eq} + K_{hous}\right)\]

\[GMP = GDP - K_{hous}\]

\[GEP = \left(\sum_{i=1}^{n} h_i \cdot w\right) + K_{eq} + K_{hous} + GDP - K_{hous} = \left(\sum_{i=1}^{n} h_i \cdot w\right) + K_{eq} + GDP\]

Variable descriptions:

Symbol Description
\(GEP\) Gross Economic Product — total value added by both household and market sectors
\(GHP\) Gross Household Product — value added by unpaid household activities
\(GMP\) Gross Market Product — GDP adjusted to remove double-counted owner-occupied housing
\(GDP\) Conventional Gross Domestic Product as measured by the SNA
\(W_H\) Value of unpaid household labor (~83% of GHP in Australia 1992)
\(h_i\) Hours worked per week in household industry \(i\) (e.g. meal prep, child care, laundry)
\(w\) Imputed wage rate per hour (AUD 14.25/hr in 1992)
\(n\) Number of household industries (8 in the paper’s classification)
\(K_H\) Total household capital contribution to GHP
\(K_{eq}\) Imputed rental value of household equipment and vehicles
\(K_{hous}\) Imputed rental value of owner-occupied housing (deducted from GDP to avoid double-counting)

The shift from household task to monetized service

  • Increasing trend of women participation in paid employment is believed by Folbre and Nelson (2000) as one aspect triggering the commodification of traditionally domestic tasks, including caring and homemaking activities
  • This is usually the case when doing more homemaking activities tends to lower parents earnings, thus indicating the increasing demand of care services due to longer paid working hours.
  • There is also another case when reallocate their non-market time and effort away from material production towards the personal and emotional actions

Factors affecting demand of paid care service (Folbre and Nelson, 2000)

Encouraging factors

  • expected freedom from doing bulk number of tedious homemaking activities
  • provide better quality of service, if:
    • caring activities seemed as matter of choice rather than necessity
    • costs being more widely and equitably distributed
    • get care services from those specially trained
  • enhance recipients’ sense of life control

Discouraging factors

  • Unable to replace personal care services that highly value the identity of provider and the continuity of the relationship
  • It is not guaranteed whether market competition will produce high quality care.
  • The market of care services may not be affordable equally across diverse groups.
  • Acute capitalization will make care services impersonal, thus produced at minimum cost and sold to the highest bidder

Factors affecting supply of paid care service (Morgan, 2005)

Encouraging factors

  • Responsibility of care is being left to private markets
  • Enormous supply of low-wage and low-skilled workers
  • Lack of unionization that could push wages higher and promote better working condition

Discouraging factors

  • Markets, even for services, are strictly controlled by government
  • Workforce rights are highly protected:
    • generous parental leave benefit
    • relatively high minimum wage
    • requirement of receiving specific vocational education
  • strong influence from worker unions
